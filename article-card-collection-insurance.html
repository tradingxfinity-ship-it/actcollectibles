Do You Need Insurance for Your Card Collection? A Complete Guide for US Collectors

TC
Tyler Chen
Hobby Culture & Guides Editor
·April 18, 2025·8 min read·✓ Expert Verified

You've spent months — maybe years — building a card collection worth thousands of dollars. You have it stored carefully, sleeved and in top loaders or slabs. But have you thought about what happens if your house floods, catches fire, or gets burglarized?

Most collectors haven't. And most homeowners' insurance policies offer very limited coverage for collectibles — often capped at $200–$500 total for all collectibles in your home, regardless of their actual value. If your collection is worth more than that (and for most serious collectors, it is), you need dedicated collectibles insurance.

What Standard Homeowners Insurance Won't Cover

Before exploring specialized coverage, understand the gaps in standard policies:

  • Low collectibles sub-limits — Most homeowners policies cap collectible coverage at $200–$500 total, regardless of your premium tier
  • Depreciation deductions — Standard policies often pay "actual cash value" (depreciated), not "replacement cost value" — for collectibles that have appreciated, this is a major shortfall
  • No accidental damage coverage — Standard policies typically only cover named perils (fire, theft, flooding). Accidentally spilling coffee on your PSA 10 Charizard isn't covered.
  • No coverage during transit — Shipping cards to PSA or to buyers isn't covered by homeowners insurance
  • No coverage at shows — Cards you bring to a card show have zero coverage if lost or stolen away from your home

Dedicated Collectibles Insurance — What It Covers

Specialized collectibles insurance policies, by contrast, are designed specifically for high-value items like sports cards, graded slabs, sealed boxes, and TCG collections. Coverage typically includes:

🛡️ What Good Collectibles Insurance Covers

  • Fire, flood, and natural disasters — Full replacement value, not depreciated ACV
  • Theft — At home, in transit, at shows, or in storage
  • Accidental damage — Drops, spills, and handling accidents included in comprehensive policies
  • Transit coverage — Cards shipped to grading companies or buyers are covered
  • Show coverage — Cards brought to card shows or conventions are protected
  • Mysterious disappearance — Some policies cover unexplained loss, not just documented theft

Top Collectibles Insurance Providers for US Card Collectors

Several insurers specialize in collectibles coverage for American collectors:

  • Collectibles Insurance Services (CIS) — The most widely used provider among card collectors. Policies from $100/year for $10,000 in coverage; rates roughly 1–1.5% of declared value annually. Easy online application and strong hobby community reputation.
  • American Collectors Insurance — Strong option for large collections; known for responsive claims handling and flexible coverage tiers
  • Chubb Masterpiece — Premium carrier favored by ultra-high-value collections ($100k+); pairs well with professional appraisals
  • Hugh Wood Inc. — Specialty fine art and collectibles insurer; excellent for mixed collections including vintage cards, signed memorabilia, and graded slabs
  • State Farm / Allstate Riders — Major insurers offer collectibles riders to existing homeowners policies; typically less comprehensive than dedicated providers but may be convenient for mid-range collections

How to Calculate How Much Coverage You Need

Determining your coverage amount requires a systematic approach:

  1. Inventory your collection — Photograph every significant card, note PSA/BGS grade if applicable, and record the card name, year, and set
  2. Check current market values — Use eBay sold listings for raw cards; PSA's SMR price guide for graded cards. Use the most recent 30-day average sale price.
  3. Total the replacement value — This is what it would cost to replace each item at current market prices, not what you paid for them
  4. Add a 15–20% buffer — Markets move; your coverage should reflect current replacement cost, not purchase price
  5. Update annually — Card values fluctuate significantly; review and update your coverage every 12 months
"Insurance is the most overlooked expense in card collecting — until you need it. I know collectors who've lost PSA 10 collections to house fires and received $250 from their homeowners policy. Don't be that collector." — Tyler Chen, ACT Collectibles

Documentation: The Step Most Collectors Skip

Insurance only pays out if you can prove what you had. Build a proper documentation file:

  • Photograph every card — Both front and back; include the PSA slab label in frame for graded cards
  • Keep purchase receipts — eBay, COMC, and LCS receipts all serve as proof of acquisition and approximate value
  • PSA and BGS certification numbers — Every graded card has a unique cert number; maintain a spreadsheet of these
  • Store documentation off-site — Cloud backup (Google Drive, iCloud) ensures your inventory list survives a house fire
  • Video walkthroughs — A short video documenting your collection storage areas is compelling supporting evidence for claims

The Cost — Is It Worth It?

Collectibles insurance typically runs 1–2% of declared value per year. For a $10,000 collection, expect to pay $100–$200 annually — less than the price of a single PSA 10 grading submission. For any collection over $2,000–$3,000 in value, dedicated insurance is almost always worth the cost relative to the risk.

Think of it this way: if a house fire destroyed your collection tomorrow, could you absorb that loss? If the answer is no, you need insurance.
