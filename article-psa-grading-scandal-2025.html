26.8 Million Cards Graded, a Buyback Fraud Scandal, and a Congressional FTC Demand: The State of Card Grading in 2025

TC
Tyler Chen
Grading & Authentication Editor · ACT Collectibles

The card grading industry in 2025 told two stories at once. The first was one of extraordinary growth: a record-shattering 26.8 million cards graded across all major companies, up 32% from 2024, with TCG submissions outpacing sports cards for the first time in history. The second story was darker: PSA — the dominant force holding 72% of the market — became engulfed in a fraud scandal in December 2025 that triggered a congressional FTC investigation demand and sent collectors scrambling for alternatives. Here's the full picture.

The Numbers: An Industry at All-Time Highs

Let's start with the good news. The overall grading market hit a record in 2025 that few analysts predicted even two years ago. The 26.8 million total cards graded represents not just a numerical record but a structural shift in what the industry looks like and who it serves.

📊
2025 Grading Industry Breakdown
PSA: 19.26M graded (+26% YoY) — 72% market share
CGC Cards: 4.92M graded (+121% YoY) — fastest growing
SGC: 1.42M graded (-24% YoY) — only major grader to decline
Beckett (BGS): 824,000+
TAG: 440,000+ (+83% YoY)
Total: 26.8M cards (+32% vs. 2024)

PSA remains the dominant player by an enormous margin, processing 19.26 million cards in 2025 — nearly four times its nearest competitor. Its market share breakdown is even more revealing when split by category: 76% of sports cards are graded by PSA, while in TCG (Pokémon, Yu-Gi-Oh!, Magic), PSA holds 69% — with CGC rapidly gaining ground at 25%.

CGC Cards was the breakout story of 2025. The company — better known for comic book grading — posted a 121% year-over-year increase in card submissions, almost entirely driven by Pokémon collectors. CGC has cultivated a loyal following among TCG enthusiasts who appreciate its grading consistency and competitive turnaround times. Its 25% share of the TCG grading market is a significant achievement in a space PSA has dominated for decades.

SGC is the cautionary tale. The company declined 24% in 2025 — the only major grader to post negative growth in a record-volume year. SGC has struggled to differentiate itself in a market where PSA's registry-driven ecosystem and CGC's modern appeal have captured most new collector attention.

The Big Structural Shift: TCG Surpasses Sports

For the first time in the history of card grading, TCG and non-sports submissions (16.8 million) outnumbered sports card submissions (10 million) in 2025. This isn't a blip — it reflects the fundamental transformation of the hobby. Pokémon TCG Pocket's extraordinary digital success drove millions of new players to physical cards. Those new collectors graduate to grading much faster than previous generations did, because platforms like Instagram, TikTok, and live breaks make graded slabs aspirational objects from day one.

The hottest rookie submissions of early 2026 tell a bifurcated story: on the sports side, Cooper Flagg, Kon Knueppel, and Jaxson Dart all saw grading volumes spike 50%+ in March 2026 as their NBA and NFL debuts drew collector attention. On the TCG side, Pokémon Scarlet & Violet Special Illustration Rares continued to dominate submission queues at every grader.

The PSA Buyback Scandal: What Happened

In December 2025, a Pokémon collector submitted approximately 30 identical modern cards to PSA for grading. The results came back mixed — most graded PSA 9. PSA then offered to purchase those PSA 9 cards through its buyback program, paying market value for PSA 9 copies at roughly $35 each. The collector, content with the offer, sold.

Then came the bombshell. After PSA completed the buyback purchases, the collector checked PSA's certification database against the cert numbers from the transaction. What he found was alarming: 11 of the cards that had been graded and purchased as PSA 9 now showed PSA 10 grades in the database — without any notification to the original owner, and without any regrading submission. Roughly 36% of that submission had been internally upgraded after purchase.

The implication was devastating: PSA had allegedly downgraded cards during initial grading, bought them at PSA 9 prices, upgraded them internally to PSA 10, and stood to re-list them at PSA 10 prices. A modern Pokémon card that grades PSA 9 might sell for $35. The same card with a PSA 10 could sell for $200 or more. The spread is enormous — and if the allegations are accurate, PSA was exploiting its position as both grader and buyer to extract value from collectors who trusted its grades.

PSA's Response and the Market Fallout

Nat Turner, CEO of Collectors Holdings (PSA's parent company), acknowledged via social media that a "systematic failure" had occurred in the process. Turner stopped short of admitting intentional fraud, framing the issue as a procedural breakdown rather than a deliberate scheme. No formal restitution program was announced publicly as of early 2026.

The market reacted quickly and harshly. PSA slab listings on eBay dropped 10–20% across the board — modern Pokémon cards saw the steepest declines, as they were the category most implicated in the scandal. Some dealers publicly announced they were halting PSA submissions entirely until the situation was resolved. Alternative graders — CGC, SGC, and TAG — reported approximately a 15% increase in new submission inquiries in the weeks following the story breaking.

Congress Weighs In: The FTC Investigation Demand

The scandal crossed from hobby news to political news in late December 2025, when Congressman Pat Ryan formally demanded an FTC investigation into PSA and its parent company Collectors Holdings. Ryan's letter cited monopoly concerns: Collectors Holdings had already acquired both SGC and Beckett (BGS), giving the company approximately 80% of the grading market across its three brands. Combined with the buyback fraud allegations, Ryan argued that collectors had no meaningful alternative grader to turn to and that the concentration of market power enabled the kind of conflict of interest alleged in the scandal.

As of early 2026, the FTC has not confirmed any formal action. The demand for investigation remains active, and the story continues to develop. For collectors, the situation raises a question that the hobby hasn't had to seriously confront before: what happens when you can't fully trust the grade on the slab?

What Collectors Should Do Right Now

The PSA scandal doesn't mean PSA grades are worthless — the vast majority of graded cards in the market are exactly what their slab says they are. But it does mean collectors should approach the market with more scrutiny, particularly for modern cards with thin margins between grade levels:

1. Verify cert numbers. PSA's cert lookup database is public. Before buying any PSA-slabbed card at a significant premium, verify the cert number at PSA.com and confirm the grade matches what's on the label.

2. Consider CGC for TCG submissions. CGC's market share growth in 2025 is evidence that the market is already diversifying away from PSA for Pokémon and other TCG cards. Their grades carry increasing marketplace credibility.

3. Buy vintage PSA grades with confidence. The scandal centered on modern cards with small per-card values where the buyback math works. Vintage graded cards — high-dollar, low-volume submissions — are far less likely to have been affected by any systematic buyback scheme.

💡
Bottom Line: The grading industry is bigger than ever, but 2025 exposed the risks of a market dominated by a single company. Competition from CGC and TAG is healthy for collectors — support graders that earn your trust, verify cert numbers before buying, and stay informed as the FTC situation develops.
