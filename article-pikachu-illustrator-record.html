$16,492,000: Logan Paul's Pikachu Illustrator Becomes the Most Expensive Trading Card Ever Sold — Anywhere, Ever

SR
Sarah Rodriguez
TCG Market Editor · ACT Collectibles

On February 16, 2026, at Goldin Auctions, a 1990s Japanese Pokémon card sold for $16,492,000 — setting the all-time record for any trading card, sports or otherwise, ever sold at public auction. The card in question: Logan Paul's PSA 10 Pikachu Illustrator, one of only 39 ever produced, and the only one in existence graded Gem Mint by PSA. A Guinness World Records official confirmed the sale on the spot. The hobby will never be the same.

What Is the Pikachu Illustrator?

To understand why this card is worth $16 million, you need to understand what it is — and how different it is from anything else in the hobby. The Pikachu Illustrator was not a product you could buy in a pack. It was not produced commercially at all. In 1998, CoroCoro Comic — a popular Japanese manga magazine — held an illustration contest asking readers to submit their own original Pokémon card artwork. The winners received an extraordinary prize: a specially created Pikachu Illustrator card, featuring a unique illustration of Pikachu holding an artist's pen, with text on the back written in Japanese identifying the card as an award for the Pokémon Card Game Illustration Contest.

Only 39 of these cards were ever distributed. That's the entire population. They were never in packs, never in sets, never sold anywhere. They were given to contest winners in Japan in the late 1990s and have since scattered to collections around the world. Of those 39, PSA has graded a handful — but only one has ever achieved a PSA 10 (Gem Mint). That's the card Logan Paul owned. The only perfect-condition example of the rarest Pokémon card ever made.

Logan Paul's Journey With the Card

Logan Paul acquired this specific card in July 2021 through a trade-plus-cash deal: he swapped a PSA 9 copy of the Pikachu Illustrator (valued at approximately $1.275 million) and added $4 million cash to another collector, paying roughly $5.275 million total. That acquisition itself was certified by Guinness as the most expensive Pokémon card transaction at the time.

Paul famously wore the card around his neck in a custom case at a boxing event, bringing enormous mainstream media attention to the Pokémon card market. For years the card became inseparable from his public persona. Then, in December 2025, he listed the card for sale at his cost basis — $5.275 million — generating another wave of media coverage. The reaction was swift: collectors and investors flooded into the conversation, driving speculation about what it might actually be worth at auction.

Paul ultimately chose to go the auction route at Goldin rather than accept any private offers. The February 16 result — $16,492,000 — represents a 312% return on his $5.275M investment in roughly four and a half years, net of any transaction costs. Not bad for a piece of cardboard.

Context: The Broader Pokémon Market in 2025-2026

The Pikachu Illustrator sale didn't happen in isolation. It came at the peak of a broad Pokémon market resurgence driven by a surprising source: a mobile game. Pokémon TCG Pocket, the digital card game app, generated a jaw-dropping $90.4 million in a single month (February 2025), introducing millions of new players to the concept of Pokémon cards as collectible objects. Many of those digital players converted to physical collectors, pouring fresh money and enthusiasm into the market.

By early 2026, TCGPlayer was reporting consistent month-over-month price increases for Pokémon cards across the board. Heritage Auctions' December 2025 TCG event set a department record with $5.28 million in sales, headlined by a 1st Edition Base Set Charizard PSA 10. Investment funds — including Alt, Rally, and Mythic Markets — have formally added high-grade Pokémon cards to their portfolios, treating them as legitimate alternative assets alongside art and wine.

Analysts now project the global trading card game market to grow from $9.2 billion in 2026 to $16.9 billion by 2035 at a compound annual growth rate of 6.9%. Pokémon holds over 12% of that market and remains the single dominant force in TCG collecting globally.

What the Record Means for Collectors

The $16.5M Pikachu Illustrator does something important for the broader hobby: it establishes a new price ceiling and validates the idea that trading cards — not just sports cards — can compete with fine art as a store of value. When a Japanese Pokémon contest prize from 1998 outperforms a Michael Jordan game-worn Logoman at public auction, the message is clear: scarcity, condition, and cultural significance can generate eight-figure prices in any collecting category.

For everyday Pokémon collectors, the lesson is more practical: pristine condition matters enormously at the high end. The difference between the world's only PSA 10 Pikachu Illustrator ($16.5M) and a PSA 9 example (valued in the $5–7M range) is not proportional to the grade difference — it's exponential. The perfect example commands a perfect premium.

💡
Collector Takeaway: True 1-of-1 or near-impossible-to-improve cards in perfect condition operate outside normal market logic. The Pikachu Illustrator PSA 10 wasn't just the best Pokémon card in the world — it was singularly irreplaceable. When that's the case, price is determined only by what someone is willing to pay, and the ceiling keeps rising.
