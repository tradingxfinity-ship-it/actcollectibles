Collectors Holdings Buys Beckett: One Company Now Controls 79% of Card Grading — And the Hobby Is Split

TC
Tyler Chen
Grading & Market Editor · ACT Collectibles

When Collectors Holdings — the parent company of PSA — announced the acquisition of Beckett Grading Services (BGS) in December 2025, the reaction across the hobby was immediate and loud. A single entity would now control PSA, SGC (acquired earlier), and BGS, accounting for roughly 79% of all grading market volume. Whether that's consolidation for the good of the hobby or the beginning of a monopoly problem depends entirely on who you ask.

The Deal and What It Includes

Collectors Holdings announced a definitive agreement to acquire Beckett on December 15, 2025. Beckett is one of the most storied names in the hobby — the company has published price guides, operated grading services, and maintained a marketplace for decades. The deal brings Beckett's entire operation under the Collectors umbrella, including BGS grading, the Beckett marketplace, Beckett magazines, and the Beckett Online Price Guide.

According to Collectors president Ryan Hoge, all three grading companies — PSA, SGC, and BGS — will continue to operate independently under their own brand identities, with their own standards, customer workflows, and culture. The shared infrastructure is meant to combat fraud more effectively, leveraging combined data across all three companies' grading histories.

⚖️
What Collectors Holdings Now Controls:
PSA — the world's largest grading company by volume
SGC — acquired earlier, focus on vintage sports cards
BGS (Beckett) — premium sub-grade system, popular for modern cards
Combined market share: ~79%

The Antitrust Question

Not everyone is comfortable with one entity owning three of the four major grading companies. Congressman Pat Ryan wrote a formal letter to the FTC urging an investigation into the acquisition shortly after the announcement. A class action lawsuit was filed in California naming Collectors, PSA, SGC, and BGS as defendants, seeking damages and forced divestment under antitrust law.

Legal experts are divided. Grading is a somewhat unique market: the value of a grade is derived from its market acceptance, not just the service itself. A PSA grade commands a premium on resale because the market has decided PSA grades are trustworthy and liquid. That kind of network effect makes the market different from, say, widget manufacturing — but it also makes control of that network incredibly powerful.

What Collectors Are Saying

The hobby community's reaction has been mixed but largely skeptical. On forums and social media, the dominant concern is pricing. With PSA, SGC, and BGS all under one roof, what happens to competitive pricing pressure? The counter-argument is that CGC — which grades comics, cards, and currency — remains a fully independent alternative, and has been growing its card grading market share steadily. The One Piece TCG community and a significant portion of the Pokémon community have been routing submissions to CGC in recent years.

Some dealers have gone further: a small but vocal group of high-volume submitters publicly boycotted PSA in early 2026 following the acquisition announcement and raised prices at the same time. Several shifted entirely to CGC and reported comparable — or in some categories, better — resale premiums.

The Combined Vision

Hoge's stated vision is a unified back-end that fights fraud more effectively across all three companies. With shared grading history data, it theoretically becomes harder for a counterfeited card to get a grade at one company after being rejected by another. That's a genuine benefit. The question is whether the benefit to authenticity outweighs the risk to price competition and consumer choice.

For now, BGS remains operationally independent. Its sub-grade system — which scores corners, edges, surface, and centering separately in addition to the overall numeric grade — still makes it the preferred choice for collectors who want the most detailed condition assessment on high-value modern cards. That's not going away.

💡
Bottom Line: The Beckett acquisition closes the largest chapter of hobby consolidation in history. BGS continues to operate as a distinct brand. CGC is the primary independent alternative. Watch the FTC — if antitrust pressure builds, a forced divestment of SGC is the most likely regulatory outcome.
